## PAGE 2 · How to get into any tool

You don't need to learn every tool. You need to open **one** and type **one** real thing. Every tool on the next pages works the same way underneath:

**The four steps that work everywhere**
1. **Open it and sign in.** A free account is plenty to start. It mostly just saves your chats.
2. **Type one real question, in plain words.** No keywords, no special syntax. "Help me write a thank-you note to my doctor" works perfectly.
3. **Read the answer like a first draft.** It's a starting point, not the final word.
4. **Ask it to try again with one change.** "Shorter." "Warmer." "For a 10-year-old." The conversation is the skill.

> Menus, free limits, and features change often. When a screen looks different from this page, trust the screen — and check the tool's official website for what's current.

## PAGE 3 · ChatGPT — the friendly all-rounder

**Made by** OpenAI. **Best for** everyday questions, writing, brainstorming, and explaining things simply.

- **Getting in:** open the official ChatGPT website (or the app). You can try it without an account; a free account saves your history.
- **What you'll see:** a large empty box, usually labelled "Message." That box is the whole thing — type there.
- **Your first conversation:** *"Explain, in plain English, what you can help me with — like I'm brand new to this."* Then try one real task from your day.
- **Good to know:** look for a small microphone (to talk instead of type) and a **+** or paperclip (to add a photo or document).
- **A gentle tip:** if an answer isn't right, don't start over — say what to change.

*Check the official site for current features and limits.*

## PAGE 4 · Claude — the careful writer

**Made by** Anthropic. **Best for** longer writing, careful or sensitive wording, and reading documents you paste or attach.

- **Getting in:** open the official Claude website and make a free account.
- **What you'll see:** a calm, simple message box, much like the others.
- **Your first conversation:** *"I'm going to paste a long email. Help me understand what it's really asking, then draft a kind, short reply."*
- **Good to know:** it tends to write in a natural, unhurried tone — helpful for hard conversations and heartfelt notes.
- **A gentle tip:** it's comfortable with long documents, so it's a good place to "explain this in plain English."

*Check the official site for current features and limits.*

## PAGE 5 · Gemini — the one that lives with Google

**Made by** Google. **Best for** tasks connected to the Google apps you may already use (mail, documents, calendar).

- **Getting in:** open the official Gemini website, or find it in the Google app, and sign in with your Google account.
- **What you'll see:** a familiar Google-style page with a message box.
- **Your first conversation:** *"Summarize the main points of this, then suggest a friendly reply."* (Paste an email or a note.)
- **Good to know:** it's most handy when the answer lives inside your Google world.
- **A gentle tip:** the exact connections change over time — try it and see what it can reach.

*Check the official site for current features and limits.*

## PAGE 6 · Copilot — the office helper

**Made by** Microsoft. **Best for** help inside work documents — writing, spreadsheets, and email.

- **Getting in:** on a Windows PC it may already be on the taskbar; there's also a website and an app.
- **What you'll see:** a side panel or a message box, often right beside the document you're working on.
- **Your first conversation:** *"Turn these rough notes into a tidy summary with three bullet points and any action items."*
- **Good to know:** a workplace version may look and behave a little differently from the personal one.
- **A gentle tip:** great for the "make this document tidier" kind of task.

*Check the official site for current features and limits.*

---

## PAGE 7 · Perplexity — the one that shows its sources

**Made by** Perplexity. **Best for** questions where you want links to real sources you can check.

- **Getting in:** open the official Perplexity website or app.
- **What you'll see:** a search-style box; answers come back with little numbered links to where the information came from.
- **Your first conversation:** *"What are the main pros and cons of [your question]? Include sources I can click and read."*
- **Good to know:** it's the closest thing to a librarian who shows you the receipts.
- **A gentle tip:** reach for it whenever a source matters more than a tidy paragraph.

*Check the official site for current features and limits.*

## PAGE 8 · Plain-English Glossary

*The words people bump into first, in plain language. You never need to memorize these — just look them up when they appear.*

- **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** — software that can understand and produce language (and sometimes images or voice) well enough to feel like a helpful assistant.
- **Chatbot** — a tool you talk to in a chat box, like texting a very well-read helper.
- **Prompt** — what you type to the AI. The clearer it is, the better the answer.
- **Prompt (writing) recipe** — a simple habit for better prompts: say who it should act as, the task, who the answer's for, the format, and the tone.
- **Model** — the "engine" underneath a tool. You don't need to know its name to use it.
- **LLM (Large Language Model)** — the kind of model behind these tools; it's very good with words, and only sometimes right about facts.
- **Generative AI** — AI that *creates* something new (a draft, a summary, a picture), rather than just finding an existing page.
- **Machine learning** — the general idea of software that improves by learning from lots of examples.
- **Training** — the long process of teaching a model from huge amounts of text before you ever use it.
- **Training cutoff** — the date after which a model hasn't "read" anything new. Ask it, or use a tool that can look things up, for very recent facts.
- **Token** — the little word-pieces a model counts. Long chats and big files use more — it's how limits and pricing are measured.
- **Context window** — how much the tool can hold in mind at once. Some read long documents; others forget after a while.
- **Hallucination** — when the AI states something untrue with full confidence. Always double-check names, numbers, dates, and sources.
- **Memory** — a feature where a tool remembers details about you between chats. Handy, but check what it's keeping.
- **Multimodal** — handles more than text: images, voice, and files in the same conversation.
- **Voice mode** — talking to the AI out loud instead of typing. Good hands-free, or if typing is tiring.
- **Agent / Agentic AI** — an AI that can take actions for you (clicking, browsing, filling things in), not just answer. Newer, and worth extra caution.
- **Free tier** — the no-cost version most tools offer, usually with daily limits. Plenty for starting out.
- **Fine-tuning** — customizing a model on specific material so it speaks a particular style or domain. (Not something a beginner needs.)
- **RAG** — a way of grounding answers in documents you provide (your PDFs, your notes) so replies stay closer to your material.
- **Open source** — models anyone can download and run themselves. More private, more setup.
- **OpenAI / Anthropic / Google / Microsoft** — companies that make the best-known tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot).
- **ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini / Copilot / Perplexity** — the five tools in this guide; see their pages above.
- **Privacy setting** — an option, often labelled something like "use my chats to improve the model." It's frequently on by default; turn it off if you're sharing anything personal.
- **Verify** — the habit of checking anything important against a reliable source before you act on it. The most valuable habit in this whole guide.
- **Draft** — the right way to think about any first answer. It's a starting point you improve, not a final result.

## PAGE 9 · Starter Reference Sheet *(print & keep)*

**The roster** — you only need one to begin
- **ChatGPT** — friendly all-rounder; everyday writing & questions.
- **Claude** — careful writer; long documents, sensitive wording.
- **Gemini** — lives with your Google apps.
- **Copilot** — help inside Office documents.
- **Perplexity** — answers with clickable sources.

**Start in four steps**
1. Open one and sign in (free is fine).
2. Type one real question, plainly.
3. Read it as a first draft.
4. Ask it to try again with one change.

**The five-part prompt** — fill in as many as help
Role · Task · Audience · Format · Tone
> *"Act as a patient tutor. Explain how a pension works. For someone with no finance background. In five short bullets. Warm and reassuring."*

**Everyday uses**
Explain a confusing letter · draft the message you're dreading · plan the week's dinners · compare two options · prepare for an appointment · make sense of messy notes.

**Stay safe**
- Never paste passwords, one-time codes, full card/bank numbers, or ID numbers.
- Swap real names/addresses for "Person A," "Street X" before sharing.
- Verify anything health, legal, or money-related with a real professional.
- AI is preparation, not a final decision. You're always the decision-maker.

## PAGE 10 · One-Page Quick-Start Cheat Sheet *(print & stick up)*

**Four tiny habits**
1. Talk to it like a person.
2. Give it the backstory (who you are, who it's for, what you want).
3. Ask it to redo, don't start over.
4. Double-check what matters.

**Stuck on what to type?** Start any sentence with:
"Help me…" · "Explain… in plain English." · "Write a short… for…" · "Compare… and…" · "What should I ask my [doctor / bank / school] about…?"

**Before you trust an answer — PAUSE**
**P**ause · **A**sk where it came from · **U**se a second source · **S**trip out private details · **E**scalate to a real person for anything serious.

**If you only do one thing this week**
Open a tool. Type one real question from your actual life. Read the answer. *You used AI today.*

## PAGE 11 · A closing note

You were never behind. Tools will keep changing — new names, new buttons, new features. It doesn't matter. The habits on these pages are the part that lasts: ask plainly, improve the answer, and check what's important. Keep this guide nearby, and come back to it whenever a screen looks unfamiliar.

*Educational guide. Not medical, legal, or financial advice. AI can be confidently wrong — verify anything important with a real source or a qualified person.*
