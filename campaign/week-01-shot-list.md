## ☐ BATCH A — Voiceover (record all 5 back-to-back, ~15 min)
Warm, unhurried, calm. Read straight from these:

- [ ] **Day 1 (94w):** "If you feel behind on AI — like everyone else got the memo and you missed it — I want to tell you something. You're not behind. You're just beginning. You don't have to learn everything. You don't have to use it every day. You don't have to catch up to anyone. You can start with one real question from your actual life — and that's enough. Open a tool, type one thing, read the answer. That's it. That's using AI. Beginning is not the same as being behind."
- [ ] **Day 2 (96w):** "If AI keeps giving you boring answers, it's probably not you — it's the question. Most of us type one line, like 'plan a workout,' and get something generic back. Good prompts have five little parts: who it should act as, the task, who the answer's for, the format, and the tone. Watch. 'Act as a gentle physiotherapist. A fifteen-minute morning workout for someone at a desk with a sore lower back. A numbered list, one rest day, warm and reassuring.' Same tool, completely different answer. Five small parts. That's the whole trick."
- [ ] **Day 3 (96w):** "People think using AI well is complicated. It's really just four small habits. One: talk to it like a person — plain, normal words, no special commands. Two: give it a little backstory — who you are, who the answer's for, what you actually want. Three: if the first answer misses, ask it to redo — shorter, warmer, whatever you need. Four: verify anything that matters — names, numbers, dates — with a real source. That's it. Talk like a person, give backstory, ask for a redo, verify. Four habits, and AI starts working for you."
- [ ] **Day 4 (89w):** "It's five o'clock, the fridge is a bit random, and nobody planned dinner. This is my favorite thing to hand to AI. I open a tool like ChatGPT or Claude and type: 'I have chicken, broccoli, rice, eggs and about thirty minutes to feed a family of four. Suggest three simple dinners, and mark the easiest one if I'm worn out.' A few seconds later I've got three real options — and it flags the low-effort one for the nights I'm done. No new groceries. No overthinking. Just dinner, sorted."
- [ ] **Day 5 (92w):** "Ever opened ChatGPT, seen that empty box, and just... stared at it? Like you needed to know the magic words first? You don't. There are no magic words. Here's your permission slip: type one real question from your actual life. Not something clever — something true. 'Explain this letter to me in plain English.' 'What's a kind way to reply to this?' 'Help me plan Tuesday.' That's it. The box isn't a test. It's just a place to ask. Type one real thing, read what comes back — and you've already started."

## ☐ BATCH B — Screen recordings (~15 min)
Record in a real tool (ChatGPT/Claude) **or** your own `ai.delightdigital.online` builder. Type only benign, non-personal text. Capture at 1080×1920 or crop a desktop capture.

- [ ] **Day 2 — before/after:** type the vague prompt `Plan a workout.` → then type the full one: `Act as a gentle physiotherapist. A 15-minute morning workout for someone at a desk with a sore lower back. A numbered list, one rest day, warm and reassuring.` → let the better answer render. Capture both.
- [ ] **Day 4 — dinner:** type `I have [chicken, broccoli, rice, eggs] and about [30 minutes] to feed [a family of 4]. Suggest 3 simple dinners, and mark the easiest one if I'm worn out.` → capture the 3-option answer with the easiest one visible.
- [ ] **Day 5 — the blank box:** capture a blinking cursor in an empty box, then slowly type, in turn: `Explain this letter to me in plain English.` → `What's a kind way to reply to this?` → `Help me plan Tuesday.`
- [ ] **Day 3 (optional 1-sec cutaway):** cursor typing `Explain this in plain English` to illustrate habit 1.
